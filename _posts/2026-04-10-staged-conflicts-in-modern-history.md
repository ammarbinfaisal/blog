## Fabricated pretexts: the incidents that launched wars

The most consequential deceptions in post-WWII history are the staged or distorted incidents used to justify military escalation. Several are now confirmed beyond dispute.

**The Gulf of Tonkin incident** stands as the most thoroughly documented case. On August 4, 1964, the Johnson administration claimed North Vietnamese torpedo boats attacked the USS Maddox for a second time in the Gulf of Tonkin. Congress passed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution 416–0 in the House and 88–2 in the Senate, authorizing full-scale military operations in Vietnam. NSA historian Robert J. Hanyok's internal study "Spartans in Darkness," declassified in 2005, concluded that NSA analysts **"made SIGINT fit the claim"** of a North Vietnamese attack—confirming deliberate intelligence distortion. The NSA's official Vietnam history, declassified in 2007, states flatly that there was no attack on August 4. At a 1997 conference, North Vietnamese General Vo Nguyen Giap told Robert McNamara directly that no second attack ever occurred. Only Senators Wayne Morse and Ernest Gruening voted against the resolution; their skepticism, dismissed at the time, proved entirely correct.

**Operation Northwoods** (1962) demonstrates that senior U.S. military officials formally proposed staging attacks on American soil to justify invading Cuba. The Joint Chiefs of Staff, under Chairman Lyman Lemnitzer, submitted plans including fake terrorist attacks in Miami and Washington, a staged shoot-down of a civilian aircraft, and fabricated evidence of Cuban aggression. President Kennedy rejected the proposal. The documents were declassified in the late 1990s and are now held at the National Archives. Though never executed, Northwoods proves that false-flag operations were not merely theoretical—they were officially proposed at the highest levels of the U.S. military.

**The Nayirah testimony** (October 10, 1990) is the most documented case of fabricated testimony used to justify military action. A 15-year-old girl identified only as "Nayirah" told the Congressional Human Rights Caucus she had witnessed Iraqi soldiers removing Kuwaiti babies from incubators and leaving them to die. The testimony was organized by Hill & Knowlton, a PR firm paid **$10.8 million** by the Kuwaiti government through a front group called Citizens for a Free Kuwait. Nayirah was later revealed to be the daughter of Kuwait's ambassador to the United States. Her father sat in the hearing room during her testimony. President Bush cited the incubator story at least ten times. Seven senators referenced it in speeches backing the use of force; the Senate vote was 52–47. Human Rights Watch concluded definitively: **"There is no truth to the charge that they stole incubators."** An investigation by Kroll Associates found Nayirah had visited the hospital for only a few minutes and saw one baby outside an incubator "for no more than a moment."

**The Lavon Affair** (1954) involved Israeli military intelligence planting bombs at American and British targets in Egypt, intending to blame the Muslim Brotherhood and destabilize Egypt's government. The operation was exposed when one of the agents' incendiary devices detonated prematurely. Israel denied involvement for decades before officially acknowledging it in 2005, when surviving agents were awarded certificates of appreciation.

## CIA coups disguised as popular uprisings

The Central Intelligence Agency orchestrated the overthrow of multiple democratically elected governments while presenting the results as organic domestic upheavals. These operations were denied for decades and are now confirmed through extensive declassification.

**Iran, 1953 (Operation TPAJAX).** The CIA and MI6 overthrew Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh after he nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company. CIA operative Kermit Roosevelt Jr. distributed hundreds of thousands of dollars to hire mobs that staged both pro-Shah and anti-Mossadegh demonstrations. The CIA simultaneously funded fake Tudeh (communist) demonstrations to discredit Mossadegh, then organized counter-protests. Declassified CIA documents confirm **"some of the most feared mobsters in Tehran were hired by the CIA"** to simulate a popular uprising. The CIA funded anti-Mossadegh newspaper articles, bribed senior clerics (confirmed by a March 2018 declassified British memo), and used the BBC to send coded messages to coup plotters. The CIA formally acknowledged its role only in 2013; the State Department released approximately 1,000 pages of detailed records in 2017. The U.S. government had denied involvement for sixty years.

**Guatemala, 1954 (Operation PBSUCCESS).** The CIA overthrew President Jacobo Árbenz using fewer than 480 rebels led by Colonel Carlos Castillo Armas, who advanced only a few miles into Guatemala. The operation succeeded through psychological warfare rather than military force. A clandestine radio station called the "Voice of Liberation," overseen by CIA officer David Atlee Phillips, broadcast fabricated reports of massive armies converging on the capital—exaggerating the invasion force by a factor of twenty-five. CIA-piloted aircraft conducted limited bombing runs to simulate overwhelming air power. The Guatemalan Foreign Minister's accurate reports of aerial bombardment were publicly denied by U.S. officials. Nick Cullather's *Secret History*, originally written as a classified CIA internal account, was later declassified and published by Stanford University Press, providing the definitive operational narrative.

**Chile, 1970–1973.** After Salvador Allende won Chile's presidential election, Nixon ordered CIA Director Richard Helms to prevent his inauguration. Helms' handwritten notes from this meeting—"make the economy scream"—became the most iconic document on CIA covert operations in Chile. The CIA spent **$8 million** on covert operations between 1970 and 1973, funding opposition media (El Mercurio newspaper received $1.6 million), political parties, strikes, and private sector organizations. The Church Committee's December 1975 report "Covert Action in Chile: 1963–1973" documented the full scope. The CIA funded both Track I (political manipulation) and Track II (military coup), though its direct operational control over the September 11, 1973 coup itself remains debated. The CIA's own 2000 Hinchey Report acknowledged "blurring of lines between monitoring coup-plotting and supporting a coup at least implicitly."

**Operation Condor** formalized cross-border assassination and repression across South America. Established November 25, 1975, the network coordinated intelligence services from Chile, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, Bolivia, and Brazil. The CIA played a key role in setting up computerized intelligence links among member states, and plans were developed at the U.S. Army School of the Americas. An estimated **60,000–80,000 people were killed** and 400,000 detained across the region. The most dramatic Condor operation on U.S. soil was the assassination of Chilean diplomat Orlando Letelier by a car bomb in Washington, D.C. on September 21, 1976. The Paraguayan "Archive of Terror"—500,000 to 700,000 pages—was discovered in 1992 and provided definitive documentary proof. In 2016, a Buenos Aires tribunal found 15 ex-military officials guilty in the first judicial confirmation that Operation Condor existed as charged.

**Indonesia, 1965–1966.** Following a failed leftist coup attempt, General Suharto launched systematic purges that killed between **500,000 and 1,000,000 people**—described by the CIA itself as "one of the worst mass murders of the twentieth century." Declassified 2017 U.S. Embassy cables revealed American officials had detailed knowledge of killings and welcomed them. The CIA provided lists of PKI (Communist Party) members to the Indonesian military, communications equipment, small arms from Thailand, and supported black propaganda radio broadcasts. As historian Bradley Simpson concluded: "Washington did everything in its power to encourage and facilitate the Army-led massacre." Declassified British Foreign Office documents (2021) revealed British propagandists secretly incited anti-communist forces.

## Cold War proxy conflicts and the art of managed escalation

The Cold War produced a distinct category of conflict: wars fought by smaller nations but shaped—in scope, duration, and outcome—by superpower decisions. The degree of "management" varied enormously, but declassified archives confirm that these were not purely organic local conflicts.

**The Korean War** was the first major "limited war" of the nuclear age. Declassified Soviet archives confirm that **Stalin gave Kim Il Sung approval to invade South Korea in late January 1950**, after initially rejecting repeated requests throughout 1949. Stalin's motivations were strategic: a 2005 discovery of a Stalin letter reveals he backed the invasion to "entangle" the United States in East Asia and "distract" America from Europe. Scholar Shen Zhihua argues Stalin's most important reason was retaining access to Port Arthur under a treaty clause allowing continued Soviet use during Asian conflicts. The war's conduct demonstrated deliberate escalation management: Truman fired MacArthur specifically to prevent expansion into a general war with China, while the Soviets secretly flew MiG-15s under Chinese and North Korean markings to avoid direct confrontation with the United States. General Omar Bradley's famous testimony—that expanding the war would be "the wrong war, at the wrong place, at the wrong time, and with the wrong enemy"—articulated the logic of managed escalation that would define Cold War strategy.

**The Vietnam War** revealed systematic deception across four administrations through the Pentagon Papers, leaked by Daniel Ellsberg in 1971. A 1965 internal document stated that **70% of the U.S. objective was "to avoid a humiliating US defeat"**—not to achieve victory. The most substantiated claim of managed outcomes involves the "decent interval" strategy. Historian Jeffrey Kimball and researcher Ken Hughes documented from Nixon White House tapes that by 1971, Nixon and Kissinger had effectively abandoned hope of military victory. Kissinger told Soviet Ambassador Dobrynin as early as May 14, 1969: "Nixon is even prepared to accept any political system in South Vietnam, provided there is a fairly reasonable interval between conclusion of an agreement and such a system." On tape, Kissinger told Nixon in August 1972: "We've got to find some formula that holds the thing together a year or two." Critics argue this strategy prolonged the war from 1969 to 1973, costing tens of thousands of additional lives for what amounted to political cover.

**The 1973 Arab-Israeli War** provides the strongest documented evidence of deliberate superpower conflict management. Declassified telephone transcripts show Kissinger told Soviet Ambassador Dobrynin during the war that **"my nightmare is a victory for either side."** Nixon and Kissinger planned to strictly regulate arms supplies to Israel to control its strategy during the fighting and heighten its dependence afterward. Most remarkably, according to Martin Indyk's research, in a May 1973 meeting with Egyptian National Security Advisor Hafez Ismail, Kissinger reportedly said: "If you want us to intervene with Israel, you'll have to create a crisis. We only deal with crisis management." After the war, Kissinger told Syrian President Assad that "military actions were necessary... the Arabs had a need to restore their honor and the Israelis needed to suffer a military failure." The war ended with both sides stopped short of decisive victory, enabling Kissinger to position the United States as sole peace broker—one of the most consequential geopolitical shifts of the Cold War.

**The Soviet-Afghan War** presents a contested case. In a 1998 interview with Le Nouvel Observateur, Zbigniew Brzezinski stated that on July 3, 1979, President Carter signed the first directive for secret aid to Afghan opposition—six months before the Soviet invasion—and that "in my opinion this aid was going to induce a Soviet military intervention." He called it "an excellent idea" that "drew the Russians into the Afghan trap." However, historian Connor Tobin's 2020 article in *Diplomatic History* dismantled the "trap" narrative using exhaustive primary sources, showing Carter's pre-invasion program was funded at a **meager $695,000**—hardly a provocation strategy. Once the Soviets invaded, however, the conflict became a deliberately managed proxy war: CIA funding through Operation Cyclone rose from $20–30 million annually in 1980 to **$630 million by 1987**, one of the longest and most expensive covert CIA operations ever.

## Post-Cold War: manufactured justifications for intervention

After the Cold War, the pattern shifted from proxy warfare to interventions justified by humanitarian claims or weapons threats—claims that often proved exaggerated or fabricated.

**The Iraq War (2003)** represents the most thoroughly investigated case of intelligence manipulation to justify military action. The Downing Street Memo of July 23, 2002—an authenticated British government document—records MI6 chief Richard Dearlove reporting after visiting Washington that **"the intelligence and facts were being fixed around the policy"** of removing Saddam Hussein. Foreign Secretary Jack Straw observed that "the case was thin." Colin Powell's February 2003 UN presentation—featuring a vial representing anthrax, diagrams of alleged mobile bio-weapons labs, and satellite imagery—was based almost entirely on fabricated or unreliable intelligence. The primary source on biological weapons was an Iraqi defector codenamed "Curveball" (Rafid Ahmed Alwan al-Janabi), handled exclusively by German intelligence, which regarded him as unreliable. No U.S. intelligence officer had interviewed him before the war. When concerns were raised within the CIA, a Counter Proliferation Unit deputy responded: **"Let's keep in mind the fact that this war's going to happen regardless of what Curve Ball said or didn't say."** Curveball later admitted to the BBC that he fabricated his claims.

The aluminum tubes claim—that Iraq was acquiring centrifuge components for uranium enrichment—was contradicted by the Department of Energy and the State Department's Bureau of Intelligence and Research, which correctly identified them as conventional rocket casings. The Niger yellowcake uranium documents were forgeries so crude that the IAEA identified them as fake "within hours"—they contained obsolete letterheads, signatures of officials no longer in office, and misspelled words. The Senate Intelligence Committee's Phase I report (2004) unanimously found the October 2002 National Intelligence Estimate was "for the most part not supported by the underlying intelligence." The Phase II report (2008) found administration public statements "were not substantiated by intelligence." The Chilcot Inquiry (2016)—a 2.6-million-word investigation—concluded Saddam "did not pose an urgent threat," intelligence was "presented with a certainty that was not justified," and "peaceful alternatives to war had not been exhausted."

**The Libyan intervention (2011)** followed a similar pattern of exaggerated threat claims. The UK Parliament's Foreign Affairs Committee report (September 2016) concluded that **"the proposition that Muammar Gaddafi would have ordered the massacre of civilians in Benghazi was not supported by the available evidence."** The committee found the government "selectively took elements of Muammar Gaddafi's rhetoric at face value," intelligence on Islamist rebel elements was "inadequate," and "the limited intervention to protect civilians had drifted into an opportunist policy of regime change." Libya went from the highest Human Development Index in Africa to a failed state.

**The Syrian civil war** produced the most contentious evidentiary disputes. The 2018 Douma chemical attack and subsequent OPCW investigation became a flashpoint when Dr. Brendan Whelan, a 16-year OPCW veteran and member of the fact-finding mission, alleged the investigation was steered toward a "preordained conclusion." Leaked documents showed a suppressed engineering assessment concluding the chlorine cylinders were "likely manually placed" rather than dropped from aircraft, and that toxicologists from a NATO-member state ruled the cause of death "inconsistent with exposure to chlorine gas." A second whistleblower, Ian Henderson, produced a separate suppressed engineering report. The Courage Foundation issued a statement signed by Noam Chomsky, Daniel Ellsberg, and four former OPCW inspectors calling on the organization to address these concerns. Counter-investigations by Bellingcat alleged the whistleblowers had connections to Russian officials. This case remains genuinely contested among credible analysts.

**The Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988)** demonstrated the United States playing both sides simultaneously. Declassified CIA and DIA documents confirm the Reagan administration provided Iraq with satellite imagery showing Iranian troop deployments while knowing Iraq would use this intelligence to direct chemical weapons attacks. More than 60 DIA officers secretly provided tactical intelligence to Iraqi forces. Meanwhile, the Iran-Contra affair revealed the administration was simultaneously selling over 1,500 missiles to Iran and illegally diverting proceeds to fund Contra rebels in Nicaragua. At the Halabja massacre (March 1988), where Iraqi forces killed 3,200–5,000 Kurdish civilians with nerve agents, the U.S. Defense Intelligence Agency initially blamed Iran. Human Rights Watch later confirmed: "Iraq carried out the attack on Halabja, and the United States, fully aware it was Iraq, accused Iran."

## Operation Gladio and Europe's strategy of tension

NATO's secret stay-behind armies represent the most extensive confirmed covert operation in Western European history, involving at least 16 countries over four decades.

On October 24, 1990, Italian Prime Minister Giulio Andreotti confirmed to the Italian Senate that a secret CIA-linked army had operated in Italy under the codename "Gladio" (Latin for "sword"), providing a list of 622 civilian members and stating that 127 weapons caches had been dismantled. Similar networks were subsequently revealed across Europe: SDRA8 in Belgium, Absalon in Denmark, LOK in Greece, Counter-Guerrilla in Turkey, P-26 in Switzerland, and comparable organizations in at least ten other countries. Coordination occurred through NATO's Clandestine Planning Committee (established 1951–52) and Allied Clandestine Committee (established 1957–58), linked to SHAPE headquarters.

The European Parliament passed a resolution on November 22, 1990, condemning the networks, noting they had **"escaped all democratic controls"** and protesting "the assumption by certain US military personnel at SHAPE and in NATO of the right to encourage the establishment in Europe of a clandestine intelligence and operation network." The resolution called on member states to dismantle all clandestine military and paramilitary networks. Only Italy, Belgium, and Switzerland carried out full parliamentary investigations. The George H.W. Bush administration refused to comment.

The critical contested question is whether these networks participated in domestic terrorism through a "strategy of tension." In Italy, the "Years of Lead" (1968–1982) saw devastating attacks including the Piazza Fontana bombing (1969, 17 killed) and the Bologna train station massacre (1980, **85 killed**). Convicted fascist Vincenzo Vinciguerra testified in 1984 about his recruitment for the 1972 Peteano bombing as part of Gladio and described "a secret force parallel to the armed forces." A 2000 Italian parliamentary report concluded that "those massacres, those bombs, those military actions had been organized or promoted or supported by men inside Italian state institutions and, as has been discovered more recently, by men linked to the structures of United States intelligence." However, a 2007 peer-reviewed analysis in the *Journal of Strategic Studies* found that "the documents released to the Parliamentary Committees do not permit the assumption that Operation Gladio was involved in any illegal activities connected with the terrorism of the late 1960s and 1970s." The direct link between the formal Gladio organization and specific terrorist acts remains a **credible minority position supported by circumstantial evidence but not definitive documentation**.

Turkey's experience was particularly extreme. The Counter-Guerrilla organization was linked to the 1955 Istanbul Pogrom, three military coups, and a 1993 parliamentary commission that identified **1,797 unsolved political murders** believed perpetrated by the network. Former Prime Minister Bülent Ecevit admitted that "a secret army had been involved in torture, massacres, assassinations and coups d'état." Turkey's experience gave rise to the modern concept of the "deep state" (*derin devlet*).

## The machinery of wartime media control

Government manipulation of media during conflicts has been documented through declassified programs, congressional investigations, and FOIA disclosures spanning the entire post-WWII era.

**CIA media influence** during the Cold War is confirmed by the Church Committee, which documented that the CIA maintained covert relationships with "several hundred foreign individuals" providing "direct access to a large number of newspapers and periodicals, scores of press services and news agencies, radio and television stations, commercial book publishers, and other foreign media outlets," at a cost of approximately **$265 million per year**. Over 1,000 books were "produced, subsidized or sponsored by the CIA before the end of 1967." Carl Bernstein's 1977 Rolling Stone investigation reported more than 400 U.S. press members secretly carried out CIA assignments. CIA Director William Colby told the committee: "Let's not pick on some poor reporters, for God's sake. Let's go to the managements. They were willing." The specific label "Operation Mockingbird"—first applied in Deborah Davis's 1979 book—is debated as a post-hoc name for a suite of activities rather than a single centrally managed program. Historian David Hadley concluded that while the conspiratorial framing is overstated, "the active role the CIA played in influencing the domestic press's output" is well-documented.

**The Pentagon Military Analyst Program** (2002–2008) is one of the most thoroughly documented modern cases of government media manipulation. David Barstow's 2008 New York Times investigation, based on **8,000 pages of FOIA documents**, revealed that over 75 retired military officers were enlisted as television surrogates to deliver administration "themes and messages" to millions "in the form of their own opinions." Internal Pentagon documents described them as **"message force multipliers."** Most had undisclosed financial ties to military contractors. Former NBC analyst Kenneth Allard called it "psyops on steroids." Analysts who deviated from talking points were dropped. Remarkably, ABC, CBS, and NBC never reported on the story. Barstow won the 2009 Pulitzer Prize for Investigative Reporting. The Pentagon Inspector General initially cleared the program, then Deputy IG Donald Horstman repudiated his own office's report, stating it "did not meet accepted quality standards."

**PR firms have played decisive roles** in shaping war narratives. Beyond Hill & Knowlton's Gulf War campaign, the Rendon Group was hired by the CIA in 1991 to "create the conditions for the removal of Hussein from power." Rendon coined the name "Iraqi National Congress," installed Ahmed Chalabi as its head, and funneled **$12 million in CIA money** to the organization. The firm coached INC defectors whose fabricated WMD stories were fed to journalists including the New York Times' Judith Miller. James Bamford's Rolling Stone investigation "The Man Who Sold the War" documented the full scope. During the Yugoslav Wars, Ruder Finn executive James Harff boasted in a 1993 interview about successfully equating Serbs with Nazis in public consciousness: "That was a tremendous coup."

**Specific fabricated narratives** have been confirmed by the participants themselves. Private Jessica Lynch testified to Congress in 2007 that the heroic combat narrative the military constructed around her capture and rescue was false—her weapon jammed and "she wasn't able to fire a single shot." The military filmed the rescue with a dedicated camera crew and distributed dramatic footage. Pat Tillman, who participated in the Lynch rescue, wrote in his journal that he believed it was "a big Public Relations stunt." Tillman himself was killed by friendly fire in 2004; the military fabricated a heroic combat death narrative, awarded a Silver Star based on falsified accounts, and ordered soldiers—including Tillman's own brother—to remain silent. The cover-up was confirmed through six investigations and congressional hearings. The toppling of Saddam's statue in Firdos Square was amplified through psyops loudspeakers and selective camera angles—wide shots revealed the square was nearly empty, perhaps 100–200 people, while CNN replayed close-up footage every 7.5 minutes. A San Francisco Chronicle reporter who filed an accurate dispatch about the small crowd had his copy rewritten by editors to describe "a jubilant crowd" that "roared its approval."

Britain maintained its own systematic propaganda apparatus. The Information Research Department operated as a covert Foreign Office propaganda wing for **29 years** (1948–1977), at its peak the largest branch of the Foreign Office with 300–600 officers. The IRD distributed anti-communist and pro-colonial material through trusted media contacts globally, produced falsified documents, and ran a secret domestic "Home Desk" that monitored "subversive" individuals. More recently, the Integrity Initiative (2015–2019), funded by the UK Foreign Office at £1.96 million annually, organized "clusters" of journalists, academics, and intelligence figures across Europe; leaked documents revealed its Twitter account attacked Labour leader Jeremy Corbyn.

Social media manipulation became institutionalized through programs like **Operation Earnest Voice**, a CENTCOM psychological operations program using sockpuppet accounts to spread pro-American messaging. A 2011 Guardian investigation revealed CENTCOM awarded a $2.76 million contract to Ntrepid Corporation for "online persona management service," enabling each operator to control up to ten fake identities simultaneously, as part of a broader $200 million program.

## From conspiracy theory to declassified fact

The historical record reveals a recurring pattern: claims dismissed as conspiracy theories are later confirmed through declassification, often decades afterward. This pattern creates genuine epistemological challenges for evaluating current claims.

The Gulf of Tonkin fabrication was dismissed as unpatriotic skepticism for forty years before NSA documents confirmed it. CIA involvement in the Iranian, Guatemalan, and Chilean coups was officially denied for decades before document releases in 2013, 2003, and 1975 respectively. MKUltra mind-control experiments were dismissed until the Church Committee confirmed them in 1975—and most files had been deliberately destroyed in 1973, with 20,000 documents surviving only because they were misfiled in a financial records building. COINTELPRO was exposed only through the 1971 burglary of an FBI field office in Media, Pennsylvania (the burglars' identities remained secret until 2014). NSA mass surveillance was dismissed as paranoid until Edward Snowden's 2013 revelations confirmed programs like PRISM.

Several significant claims remain in a contested middle ground. The PNAC think tank's September 2000 report stating that military transformation would be slow "absent some catastrophic and catalyzing event—like a new Pearl Harbor" is documented fact; ten PNAC signatories joined the Bush administration. The claim that 9/11 was engineered to realize PNAC's agenda, however, lacks supporting evidence from any investigation. Theories about ISIS being directly created by Western intelligence are traced by PolitiFact to a 2014 Arabic-language blog post falsely attributed to Snowden, whose lawyer called it "a hoax." The more defensible "blowback" argument—that U.S. policy decisions including the 2003 invasion, disbanding of the Iraqi military, and CIA training of Syrian rebels created conditions enabling ISIS's rise—is supported by substantial evidence but is categorically different from a direct-creation claim.

Academic study of these phenomena has produced sophisticated frameworks. Peter Dale Scott's concept of "deep politics"—"all those political practices and arrangements, deliberate or not, which are usually repressed rather than acknowledged"—provides analytical tools for examining structural patterns without requiring identification of individual villains. Lance deHaven-Smith's *Conspiracy Theory in America* (Cambridge University Press) traces how the CIA's 1967 dispatch "Concerning Criticism of the Warren Report" contributed to weaponizing the term, though claims that the CIA "invented" the phrase are false—it dates to at least 1863. Richard Hofstadter's "paranoid style" framework identifies a pattern of seeing conspiracy as "the motive force in historical events," while critics like Jesse Walker argue that "the paranoid style IS American politics" across the entire political spectrum.

The CIA's 1967 document itself is revealing: labeled "PSYCH" and "CS" (Clandestine Services), it recommended approaching "friendly elite contacts, especially politicians and editors" to defend official narratives. The document's existence, confirmed through FOIA, illustrates precisely the institutional behavior that generates justified skepticism.

## Managed conflict as geopolitical practice

The concept of deliberately managed warfare is not conspiracy theory but mainstream international relations scholarship. President Eisenhower's 1961 warning about "the acquisition of unwarranted influence by the military-industrial complex" launched an academic tradition that now includes C. Wright Mills' *The Power Elite*, the stability-instability paradox in nuclear deterrence theory, and RAND Corporation analyses of proxy warfare motivations.

The strongest evidence for managed conflict comes from the Cold War's nuclear constraints. The RAND Corporation's 2022 study *Proxy Warfare in Strategic Competition* concluded that nuclear deterrence fundamentally changed warfare—"states can no longer maximize their security outcomes by engaging in state-to-state military conflicts" under mutually assured destruction. This drove superpowers toward proxy conflicts where scope and outcomes could be calibrated. The Korean War's "limited war" doctrine, Kissinger's documented management of the 1973 Arab-Israeli War, and the Vietnam War's "decent interval" strategy all demonstrate this pattern with primary-source evidence.

However, the evidence consistently shows that **"managed" does not mean "orchestrated."** Local actors retained significant agency and often drove events in directions superpowers did not intend. Cuba intervened in Angola independently before securing full Soviet backing. The Ogaden War saw superpower alignments completely reverse when Ethiopia switched from American to Soviet allegiance. Kim Il Sung persistently lobbied Stalin for permission to invade South Korea—he was not merely executing Moscow's orders. The academic consensus, articulated by scholars like Odd Arne Westad and Abbas Farasoo, holds that proxy wars involved complex interplay between superpower interests and local dynamics, not puppet-master control.

The "wag the dog" phenomenon—using military action for domestic political purposes—is well-established as a concept but difficult to prove in specific cases. The most cited instances include Clinton's 1998 cruise missile strikes during the Lewinsky scandal and Trump's 2020 assassination of Iranian General Suleimani during impeachment proceedings. Political scientist Robert Ovetz notes that "only strong direct evidence, such as internal communications and testimonies, elevate the 'wag the dog' theory from a conspiracy theory to a real scandal." Timing correlation alone proves nothing.

## What these patterns reveal

The documentary record leads to several conclusions that are neither reassuring nor conspiratorial.

First, **the gap between public justification and actual motivation for military action has been enormous and systematic.** The Pentagon Papers, the Downing Street Memo, the Senate Intelligence Committee reports, and the Chilcot Inquiry all document this gap with primary sources across different conflicts, administrations, and countries. This is not a pattern that depends on speculation.

Second, **the mechanisms of deception have evolved but not disappeared.** The clandestine radio stations of 1954 Guatemala became the Pentagon Military Analyst Program of 2002; Hill & Knowlton's 1990 testimony coaching became the Rendon Group's defector pipeline; Operation Mockingbird's journalist recruitment gave way to embedded journalism's structural bias and Operation Earnest Voice's sockpuppet armies. The tools change; the institutional incentive to control war narratives does not.

Third, **declassification remains the single most important mechanism for resolving contested claims.** Every major revelation discussed in this report—from the Gulf of Tonkin to Operation Condor to CIA coups to COINTELPRO—came through document releases, whether through congressional investigation, FOIA requests, whistleblower disclosures, or scheduled declassification. The National Security Archive at George Washington University has been instrumental in this process. Claims that remain unresolvable today may be resolved by future declassification; claims that resist all evidence regardless of what is released are likely unfounded.

Fourth, **the distinction between "managed" and "orchestrated" matters profoundly.** The evidence supports the conclusion that powerful states routinely shape, prolong, and manipulate conflicts for strategic advantage. It does not support the conclusion that all wars are theater performed for a hidden audience. Real people fought and died in every conflict discussed here, and local grievances, ambitions, and agency were genuine even when exploited by outside powers. The most productive analytical approach—exemplified by scholars like Peter Dale Scott and institutions like the National Security Archive—combines rigorous evidence standards with institutional skepticism, recognizing that governments conspire without concluding that all events are conspiracy.

The governments that most aggressively deployed the "conspiracy theory" label to dismiss critics were later proven to have been concealing real conspiracies. This historical pattern warrants neither paranoia nor credulity—but it demands that the burden of proof rest on those who claim transparency, not on those who question official narratives.
